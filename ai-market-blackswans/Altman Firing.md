## Event 4: OpenAI Board Crisis / Altman Firing (November 2023) — The Governance Shock

### 1. Vectors Hit
* **Financial / Corporate Governance:** A massive governance failure demonstrating the extreme fragility of a 501(c)(3) non-profit board controlling a hyper-valuable commercial entity (valued at $86B at the time). It almost vaporized the world's leading AI company over a single weekend.
* **Tech Discontinuity:** Dragged the internal Silicon Valley battle over "AGI alignment/safety" vs. "commercial accelerationism" directly into the public sphere, highlighting a high risk of technology lockup depending on who controls the boardroom.
* **Regulatory / Geopolitical:** Exposed the fragility of the U.S. AI ecosystem. Lawmakers and national security officials realized the primary engine of American AI dominance rested on the unvetted whims of a six-person non-profit board.

### 2. Key Actors Affected
* **Felt it first:** Microsoft (CEO Satya Nadella), whose multi-billion dollar investment and entire product roadmap were suddenly in existential jeopardy without advance warning. Also, OpenAI employees who faced instant equity devaluation and career chaos.
* **Felt it hardest:** The original non-profit board members (Ilya Sutskever, Helen Toner, Tasha McCauley, Adam D'Angelo). Their attempt to exercise legal authority failed completely; they faced immense industry backlash, an employee mutiny (95%+ threatened to quit), and ultimately lost control of the company.
* **Insulated:** Competitors like Anthropic, Google, and open-source models (Llama). Over that chaotic weekend, enterprise clients immediately began diversifying their API dependencies to avoid single points of failure, directly benefiting OpenAI's rivals.

### 3. Precursor Signals
* **Hinton's Departure & Ilya's Shift:** OpenAI Chief Scientist Ilya Sutskever was deeply influenced by his former mentor Geoffrey Hinton's pivot to existential risk, leading Sutskever to form the "Superalignment" team in July 2023 and aggressively push back on deployment speed.
* **Structural Mismatch:** The fundamental friction of an organizational chart where a non-profit board had an absolute fiduciary duty to "all of humanity" rather than commercial investors, yet oversaw a rapidly scaling, profit-capped tech behemoth demanding immense capital.
* **Altman's Side-Ventures:** Rumors of Sam Altman seeking billions from Middle Eastern investors for an AI chip venture (Project Tigris) and a hardware device (with Jony Ive), creating friction with the board over his focus and transparency.

### 4. Shock Duration
* **Days (Acute) to Months (Structural).** The acute crisis resolved in five days with Altman’s reinstatement. The structural aftermath—replacing the board with traditional corporate heavyweights (Larry Summers, Bret Taylor), dissolving the Superalignment team, and beginning the legal transition to a traditional for-profit corporate structure—took over a year to finalize.

### 5. Recurrence & Swan Classification
* **Black Swan (Corporate) / Grey Swan (Ideological).** The underlying tension between safety advocates and commercial builders was a known Grey Swan. However, the *mechanics*—a Friday afternoon firing of the most prominent tech CEO in the world by his own non-profit board without warning major shareholders like Microsoft—was an absolute Black Swan event in corporate governance.
