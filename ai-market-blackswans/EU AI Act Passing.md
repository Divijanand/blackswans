## Event 6: EU AI Act Passing (2024) — The Regulatory Shock

### 1. Vectors Hit
* **Regulatory / Policy:** The establishment of the world’s first comprehensive, legally binding, extra-territorial framework for AI. It codified a strict risk-based classification system, carrying catastrophic financial penalties for non-compliance (up to 7% of global annual turnover).
* **Financial:** Imposed massive structural compliance and auditing costs on foundation model creators, directly shifting venture capital risk profiles away from companies unable to afford multi-million-dollar legal overhead.
* **Tech Discontinuity:** Enforced strict documentation and copyright transparency on "General Purpose AI" (GPAI) models. This directly challenged the industry's culture of architectural secrecy and complicated the deployment of open-source weights within European borders.

### 2. Key Actors Affected
* **Felt it first:** US-based frontier labs (OpenAI, Google, Meta, Anthropic) and their corporate compliance teams, who had to immediately evaluate whether to alter their global technical architectures or risk facing massive European fines.
* **Felt it hardest:** European tech startups (such as Mistral AI and Aleph Alpha). Despite heavy lobbying, they found themselves bound by systemic obligations that restricted their ability to rapidly iterate against well-capitalized, unconstrained American or Chinese competitors.
* **Insulated:** Massive legacy enterprise software incumbents. With mature corporate legal departments and large capital reserves, they readily absorbed the compliance costs, turning regulatory hurdles into a defensive moat against smaller, agile startups.

### 3. Precursor Signals
* **The Original 2021 Draft:** The EU had published its first draft of the AI Act long before the generative AI boom, focusing on narrow classifications like facial recognition. The intent to regulate was clearly signaled years in advance.
* **The "Brussels Effect" (GDPR, 2016):** The European Union had already demonstrated its playbook with data privacy. Anyone tracking global tech knew the EU would aggressively seek to set the global regulatory baseline for AI using the same extra-territorial mechanics.
* **The Late-2023 Political Scramble:** Following the launch of ChatGPT, EU lawmakers frantically added specific, late-stage amendments targeting foundation models, providing a clear 6-month warning that GPAI was going to be heavily constrained.

### 4. Shock Duration
* **Years.** Because the Act operates on a tiered enforcement timeline—phasing out banned practices first, followed by foundation model rules, and finally high-risk system classifications—the market was forced into a rolling, multi-year compliance shock that continues to reshape deployment strategies.

### 5. Recurrence & Swan Classification
* **White Swan.** This event is a textbook example of predictable bureaucratic execution. The EU acts as a regulatory superpower; its intervention was structurally inevitable. The only slight Grey Swan element was the chaotic political brinkmanship required to force-fit conversational LLMs into a regulatory architecture originally designed for predictive algorithms.
