## Event 1: App Store Antitrust Ruling — Epic v Apple (2021 Onwards) — The Walled Garden Fracture

### 1. Vectors Hit
* **Regulatory / Legal:** A direct, existential challenge to Apple’s "anti-steering" rules and its absolute control over the iOS closed-ecosystem payment monopoly. It triggered a compounding legal cascade spanning US Federal Courts and echoing into European antitrust enforcement.
* **Financial:** Threatened the core mechanism of Apple’s Services revenue (the 15–30% "Apple Tax" on digital goods), forcing the company to spend years litigating to protect tens of billions in annual high-margin profit.
* **Tech Discontinuity:** Pushed the concept of "App2Web" commerce—forcing the transition of digital checkout flows from native iOS UI to external web processors (Merchant of Record solutions).

### 2. Key Actors Affected
* **Felt it first:** Epic Games (who saw *Fortnite* instantly banned from the App Store, vaporizing hundreds of millions in mobile revenue) and Apple's executive team, who faced a highly coordinated PR and legal ambush.
* **Felt it hardest:** The broader mobile subscription and gaming developer class (e.g., Spotify, Match Group). They saw a legal pathway to bypass Apple's tax but were dragged into a half-decade of judicial limbo, dealing with retaliatory compliance measures like Apple's heavily contested 27% external link fee.
* **Insulated:** Pure physical-goods commerce apps (Amazon, Uber, DoorDash). Because Apple's In-App Purchase (IAP) tax only applies to digital goods and services, the physical commerce sector was a passive observer entirely unaffected by the ruling.

### 3. Precursor Signals
* **Spotify’s EU Complaint (2019):** Spotify formally filed an antitrust complaint with the European Commission over the 30% tax and anti-steering rules long before Epic sued in the US.
* **The "Hey" Email Controversy (June 2020):** Just months prior to Epic's trap, Apple threatened to remove the Basecamp-built email app *Hey* for not offering in-app signups, triggering a massive, public developer revolt over App Store policies.
* **Growing Antitrust Scrutiny:** Congress and global regulators had increasingly begun hauling tech CEOs to Washington to testify about platform monopolies throughout 2019 and 2020. 

### 4. Shock Duration
* **Years (Compounding Long-Tail).** This event began as a discrete shock in August 2020 with the "Nineteen Eighty-Fortnite" stunt. However, it evolved into a permanent, rolling war. Following the 2021 ruling, it escalated into a 2025 civil contempt finding against Apple for instituting a 27% "anti-steering" fee, which the 9th Circuit upheld in late 2025. By mid-2026, it remained an active Supreme Court skirmish while simultaneously empowering the EU's €500M fine against Apple for similar violations.

### 5. Recurrence & Swan Classification
* **Grey Swan (Legal) / Black Swan (Execution).** Antitrust lawsuits against platform monopolies are a historical standard (e.g., US v. Microsoft). However, Epic’s *execution*—deliberately hotfixing a payment bypass into the world's most popular game, waiting for the inevitable ban, and instantly launching a pre-rendered, global 1984-themed marketing campaign alongside a pre-written 60-page lawsuit—was an absolute Black Swan in corporate warfare.
